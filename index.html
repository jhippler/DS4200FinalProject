Introduction

Alzheimer’s disease is a leading cause of dementia and a growing global health concern. In North America, the increasing prevalence of Alzheimer’s is closely tied to aging populations, lifestyle patterns, and access to healthcare. This project is driven by a simple but urgent question: Who is most at risk for Alzheimer’s disease, and why?

Using a data science approach, we aim to explore diagnosis patterns across demographics, health behaviors, and environmental conditions to better understand how Alzheimer's risk varies within and across countries. Our visualizations are designed to highlight disparities and trends in the data and help guide future public health research and early intervention strategies.

Data Description

We use the Alzheimer’s Prediction Dataset (Global), sourced from Kaggle. This dataset provides a global perspective on Alzheimer’s risk factors and prevalence. It contains 74,283 records from 20 countries and includes a wide range of variables related to Alzheimer’s disease, with a biased distribution to reflect real-world disparities across regions.

The dataset consists of 25 columns and is well-suited for predictive modeling, epidemiological studies, and healthcare research. Key attributes include:

  • Demographic: Country, Age, Gender, Education Level
  • Lifestyle: Physical Activity Level, Smoking Status, Alcohol Consumption, Sleep Quality, Stress Level, Dietary Habits, Social Engagement
  • Medical: BMI, Diabetes, Hypertension, Cholesterol Level, Depression Level, Cognitive Test Score
  • Genetic/Family History: Genetic Risk Factor (APOE-ε4 allele), Family History of Alzheimer’s
  • Environmental & Socioeconomic: Air Pollution Exposure, Income Level, Employment Status, Marital Status, Urban vs Rural Living
  • Outcome: Alzheimer’s Diagnosis (binary label)

The dataset is 100% complete with no missing or mismatched values, and offers a comprehensive foundation for exploring the intersection of personal, genetic, and societal factors in Alzheimer’s diagnosis.
